Chapter 1 · The mental model

How Spanish verbs work

Every Spanish verb starts life as an infinitive — the “to ___” form. You can spot it instantly because it ends in one of three ways:

-arhablar · to speak-ercomer · to eat-irvivir · to live

A conjugated verb is just two pieces glued together:

Stem + ending

habl (the stem — the meaning)  +  -o (the ending — who's doing it)  =  hablo (“I speak”).

The ending does a job English needs a pronoun for. That's why Spanish can drop the pronoun: hablo already means I speak.

The five people you'll use (subject pronouns)

yoI
túyou (friendly)
él / ella / ustedhe / she / you (polite)
nosotroswe
ellos / ellas / ustedesthey / you all
Two quick notes for real life

tú vs usted: use tú with friends, family, your boyfriend; usted to be respectful with strangers or elders.
ustedes: in Cuba and all of Latin America this is the only “you all,” so this book leaves out Spain's vosotros entirely — one less form to learn.

The four questions to ask every new verb

This is the checklist from your notes, and it's the whole game. When you meet any verb, run down this list:

  1. Is it regular? If yes, just add the normal endings. Done.
  2. Is only the yo form irregular? (hacer → hago) The rest are normal.
  3. Does the stem change? (querer → quiero) These are the “boot” verbs.
  4. Is it completely irregular? (ser, ir) A short list you memorize like song lyrics.
Why this saves you

Most verbs are regular or only-yo-irregular. The truly wild ones are a tiny club. Sort first, memorize second — you'll do a fraction of the work.

Chapter 3 · The first irregular pattern

Pattern A — only the “yo” form is irregular

Pattern A · yo is the odd one

The friendliest irregulars: only the yo form misbehaves. Every other form is perfectly regular. Two main flavors:

The two flavors

“-go” verbs: the yo form grows a g — hacer → hago, poner → pongo, salir → salgo.
“-zco” verbs: verbs ending in -cer/-cir → conocer → conozco.

hacer — to do / to make

yohago
túhaces
él / ella / ustedhace
nosotroshacemos
ellos / ellas / ustedeshacen
Hook

Everything is regular except hago — the yo form grows a 'g'.

  • 01Hago ejercicio en la playa por la mañana.I do exercise at the beach in the morning.
  • 02¿Qué haces este fin de semana?What are you doing this weekend?

poner — to put / to set

yopongo
túpones
él / ella / ustedpone
nosotrosponemos
ellos / ellas / ustedesponen
Hook

Only pongo is irregular — I 'plug in' a g.

  • 01Pongo música cuando cocino.I put on music when I cook.
  • 02Pongo la tabla de remo en el carro.I put the paddleboard in the car.

salir — to leave / to go out

yosalgo
túsales
él / ella / ustedsale
nosotrossalimos
ellos / ellas / ustedessalen
Hook

Only salgo grows the g.

  • 01Salgo a correr al amanecer.I go out to run at dawn.
  • 02Salgo con mi novio el sábado.I'm going out with my boyfriend on Saturday.

Hook

Yo form becomes -zco: conozco. (Verbs ending in -cer/-cir do this.)

  • 01Conozco un café cubano increíble.I know an amazing Cuban café.
  • 02¿Conoces La Habana?Do you know / Have you been to Havana?

ver — to see / to watch

túves
él / ella / ustedve
nosotrosvemos
ellos / ellas / ustedesven
Hook

Slightly odd yo form: veo (keeps the e).

  • 01Veo delfines desde la tabla de remo.I see dolphins from the paddleboard.
  • 02¿Ves esa planta? Necesita agua.Do you see that plant? It needs water.

Hook

Yo form ends in -oy: doy (like voy, soy, estoy).

  • 01Doy un paseo con el perro cada tarde.I take a walk with the dog every afternoon.
  • 02Te doy un beso.I give you a kiss.

saber — to know (facts/how to)

yosé
túsabes
él / ella / ustedsabe
nosotrossabemos
ellos / ellas / ustedessaben
Hook

Only the yo form is odd: sé (with an accent, to look different from 'se').

  • 01Sé nadar muy bien.I know how to swim very well.
  • 02¿Sabes la dirección del gimnasio?Do you know the address of the gym?
Watch out

Because only yo changes, the temptation is to change the others too. Don't — it's hago but haces, hace, hacemos, hacen (all regular).

Chapter 4 · The boot

Pattern B — stem-changing “boot” verbs

Pattern B · the stem changes

In these verbs the vowel inside the stem changes — but only in the forms that fall inside the “boot.” The nosotros form sits outside the boot and stays flat. (Teachers call it the “boot” because with all six persons written out, the changed forms trace a boot shape.)

The rule in one line

The stem changes everywhere except nosotros. So if you can conjugate yo and nosotros, you know the whole verb.

The boot, shaded (querer, e→ie)

túquieres
él/ella/ud.quiere

ellos/uds.quieren

Attack plan for ANY new verb

1) Try the regular endings. 2) Fix the yo form if it's a -go/-zco verb. 3) Check for a boot stem change. 4) If it's ser/estar/ir/haber, you already know it. That's it — four moves cover almost everything.

Chapter 7 · The famous pair

Ser vs. Estar — two ways to “be”

Spanish has two verbs for “to be,” and choosing between them is one of the biggest early wins. The short version:

The one-line rule

SER = the essence — what something fundamentally is. ESTAR = the state — how/where something is right now.

SER — identity & essence

Identity — who/what something is
Soy Celeste. Él es mi novio. — I'm Celeste. He's my boyfriend.

Origin — where you're from
Soy de Florida. Él es de Cuba. — I'm from Florida. He's from Cuba.

Permanent traits
El café cubano es dulce. — Cuban coffee is sweet.

Profession
Soy instructora de yoga. — I'm a yoga instructor.

Time & dates
Son las ocho. Hoy es martes. — It's eight o'clock. Today is Tuesday.

What something's made of
La tabla es de fibra de vidrio. — The board is made of fiberglass.

ESTAR — state, place & feeling

Location — where something is
Estoy en la playa. El perro está en casa. — I'm at the beach. The dog is home.

Feelings & moods
Estoy feliz hoy. — I'm happy today.

Temporary states
El agua está fría. — The water is cold (right now).

Health
Estoy cansada después del gimnasio. — I'm tired after the gym.

-ando / -iendo (in progress)
Estoy cocinando. — I'm cooking.

Result of a change
La puerta está abierta. — The door is open.

Same word, different verb, different meaning

With SERWith ESTAR
Él es aburrido.He is boring (personality).Él está aburrido.He is bored (right now).
Ella es guapa.She is good-looking (always).Ella está guapa hoy.She looks great today.
El café es rico.Coffee is delicious (in general).El café está rico.This coffee tastes great.
Estoy lista.— (with estar) I'm ready.Soy lista.I'm clever/smart.
A memory trick — “How you feel and where you are, always use ESTAR.”

Feelings and location = estar. Almost everything permanent = ser.

Chapter 8 · Doing things to yourself

Reflexive verbs — your daily routine

Reflexive verbs describe actions you do to yourself — wake up, get up, shower, get dressed. You spot them by the -se stuck on the infinitive: levantarse. To use one, you move that little pronoun to the front and conjugate normally.

The reflexive pronouns

me (myself) · te (yourself) · se (him/herself) · nos (ourselves) · se (themselves / yourselves)

So levantarse → me levanto = “I get (myself) up.”

Put your morning in Spanish

Chapter 9 · Likes, loves, and aches

Gustar & friends — “backwards” verbs

Here's the surprise: gustar doesn't really mean “to like.” It means “to be pleasing.” So me gusta el café literally says “coffee is pleasing to me.” The thing you like is the subject — so the verb agrees with it, not with you.

The two shapes you need

me gusta + one thing / an action → Me gusta la playa. Me gusta nadar.
me gustan + plural things → Me gustan los perros.

The pronouns that go in front

meto me
teto you
leto him/her/you-formal
nosto us
lesto them/you-all

The whole family works the same way

gustar — to like (lit. to be pleasing)
Me gusta la playa. Me gustan los perros. — I like the beach. I like dogs.

encantar — to love (things)
Me encanta el café cubano. — I love Cuban coffee.

doler — to hurt
Me duelen los pies después de correr. — My feet hurt after running.

faltar — to be lacking / to need
Me falta practicar más. — I need to practice more.

importar — to matter
No me importa la lluvia. — The rain doesn't matter to me.

interesar — to be interesting to
Me interesa la cultura cubana. — Cuban culture interests me.

Chapter 10 · -ando / -iendo

Right now — the present progressive

To stress that something is happening at this very moment, use estar + the -ando/-iendo form (English “-ing”). You already know estar from Chapter 7 — now just bolt on the gerund.

Build it in two steps

1) Conjugate estar (estoy, estás, está…). 2) Add the gerund: -ar → -ando, -er/-ir → -iendo.
Estoy + nadando = Estoy nadando — “I am swimming.”

Forming the gerund

escribirescribiendoregular -ir → -iendo

  • 01Estoy nadando en el mar.I am swimming in the sea.
  • 02Estás hablando muy rápido.You are talking very fast.
  • 03Ella está cocinando arroz con frijoles.She is cooking rice and beans.
  • 04Estamos escuchando música cubana.We are listening to Cuban music.
  • 05El perro está durmiendo en el sofá.The dog is sleeping on the couch.
Don't overuse it

Unlike English, Spanish uses the plain present for habits and near-future plans. “I'm working tomorrow” is Trabajo mañana, not the progressive. Save estoy -ando for “right this second.”

Chapter 11 · Your first past tense

Stepping into the past

Big moment: your first tense beyond the present. Spanish has two everyday past tenses, and the trick is knowing which job each one does.

Two pasts, two jobs

Preterite = a snapshot. One finished action, done and over. “I swam.”
Imperfect = a video / background. What used to happen, or what was going on. “I used to swim / I was swimming.”

PastThe preterite (finished actions)

Regular verbs — take off the ending and add:

The yo and él forms carry a stressed accent: hablé, habló. And -er/-ir share the exact same endings here — one set to learn, not two.

Which past do I use? A decision guide

If you mean…UseExample
A single, completed actionPRETERITEAyer fui a la playa.
Yesterday I went to the beach.
What happened next (a sequence)PRETERITELlegué, nadé y comí.
I arrived, swam, and ate.
A sudden interruptionPRETERITESonó el teléfono.
The phone rang.
Used-to / habitual in the pastIMPERFECTDe niña nadaba cada día.

Background, weather, time, ageIMPERFECTEran las ocho y hacía calor.

The classic combo

They often appear together: the imperfect sets the scene, the preterite drops the event in. Yo cocinaba (background) cuando mi novio llegó (event). — “I was cooking when my boyfriend arrived.”

Chapter 12 · Two ways to say tomorrow

Stepping into the future

Two ways forward, and the first one is the easiest tense in the whole book — you already know it.

FutureThe easy way: ir a + infinitive

You already have this

Conjugate ir (voy, vas, va, vamos, van), add a, then drop in any infinitive — no new endings to learn.
Voy a nadar = “I'm going to swim.”

  • 01Voy a nadar esta tarde.I'm going to swim this afternoon.
  • 02¿Vas a venir a la playa?Are you going to come to the beach?
  • 03Vamos a cocinar juntos esta noche.We're going to cook together tonight.
  • 04Mi novio va a visitar a su familia.My boyfriend is going to visit his family.
  • 05Va a llover mañana.It's going to rain tomorrow.
This is how people actually talk

In everyday Cuban and Latin-American speech, ir a + infinitivo is the go-to future. If you only learn one, learn this.

Chapter 13 · Putting it together

One verb, three times

Here's the payoff. The same verb, walked across time. Read each row left to right and feel the shape of past → present → future.

nadar
PastAyer nadé.I swam.
NowHoy nado.I swim / I'm swimming.
FutureMañana voy a nadar / nadaré.I'm going to swim / I will swim.
comer
PastAnoche comí.I ate.
NowAhora como.I eat / I'm eating.
FutureLuego voy a comer / comeré.I'm going to eat / I will eat.
ir
PastFui a la playa.I went to the beach.
NowVoy a la playa.I go to the beach.
FutureVoy a ir / iré a la playa.I'm going to go / I'll go to the beach.
hacer
PastHice café.I made coffee.
NowHago café.I make coffee.
FutureVoy a hacer / haré café.I'm going to make / I'll make coffee.
Your practice habit from here

Pick any verb you know and say it three ways out loud: what you did yesterday, what you do today, what you'll do tomorrow. Do it with five verbs and you're conjugating across all three tenses.
